The White-Label Web Design Playbook for Agencies

How to deliver AI-generated websites entirely under your own brand — from client portal to invoice — using white-label tools available on every plan.

ApexSites Team·11 min read

White-labeling is the difference between reselling a tool and running an agency. When every touchpoint — the proposal, the build, the client login, the invoice — carries your brand instead of your software vendor's, clients see a professional web design partner, not a reseller. That perception is what lets you charge agency rates instead of marketplace rates.

What "white-label" should actually cover

A complete white-label setup goes beyond swapping a logo. It should cover:

  • Your branding on the client-facing portal — the dashboard a client uses to review revisions or approve a design.
  • Your domain for the delivered site, not a subdomain of the platform you build on.
  • Your contact details on every email notification the client receives about their project.
  • Your invoice and payment flow, so the client's billing relationship is with your agency.

ApexSites includes white-label branding on every plan, including Starter, specifically so a solo freelancer's first client gets the same brand-consistent experience as an established agency's fiftieth.

Why white-label matters more once you resell hosting

Hosting is one of the best recurring-revenue add-ons in this business, but it only works as a margin driver if the client believes they're paying you for it — not routing a monthly fee to a third-party platform they've never heard of. A consistent white-label setup, paired with your own client portal, makes the monthly hosting charge feel like a natural extension of the relationship rather than a surprise line item.

Setting client expectations without over-explaining the tooling

You don't need to hide that you use AI-assisted tools — most clients don't ask, and the ones who do usually just want reassurance that a real person is reviewing the output. Focus the conversation on turnaround time, revision process, and what's included, not on the mechanics behind the build. See our guide on presenting AI websites to clients for exact language that keeps the focus on outcomes.

A simple white-label rollout checklist

  • Connect your own domain (or the client's) before you send the first preview link.
  • Update the client portal branding — logo, color, and support contact — before onboarding your first paying client.
  • Decide your hosting markup ahead of the sales call so it's a confident, pre-set number rather than something you improvise.
  • Template your proposal and invoice so every new client gets a consistent, branded experience from quote to delivery.
